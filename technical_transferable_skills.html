Evidence of Technical and Transferable Skills

The CARL framework was used to structure this reflection!

Certificates

Content

While working as an intern student at Securestack company as part of my work placement, I encountered professional challenges with both technical and transferable skills. In terms of technical skills, I had to convert Bash script penetration testing logic to Python. First, I thought it was easy to accomplish since I already knew and made some projects in Python, and all I needed to do was to translate syntaxes from Bash to Python. Yet I was totally wrong because my project focused more on logic and how to run Bash penetration testing tools within Python. Though my prior knowledge in Python had helped me a lot with this project, I started to struggle with my tasks from week 4 of this placement. Time management skill was also one of my concerned. I could not balance between my schoolwork and the placement. This made me fail behind the assignment deadline. In addition, I lacked problem-solving skills. I noticed that at the beginning of the placement whenever I got stuck with my codes, I would delegate some time to find the solution; if I could not find it, I would go directly to ask my supervisor for help. It was not a good practice to always get the answers from the supervisor. I knew I needed to be changed, and I had to do something about it, or I would not make it for this trimester.


Action

For the sake of personal development, I had developed good time management skills, especially for combining studying with the work placement demands. First, I would start doing the task that was important and urgent first so that I would not rush to submit my assignment or project; then I would do the important work that was not so urgent. With this strategy, I eliminated unimportant tasks and focused on the big picture. Since then, I had more time to learn new skills related to my project, such as Git, Docker Engine, FastAPI, and PostgreSQL. Having discussed my issue with my supervisor at Securestack, I was able to develop strategies on how to solve my coding problems with penetration testing tools as well. Though I still went to him for some advice during the placement, the numbers had cut down a lot.


Result

With the support and mentor of my supervisor, I was able to change and improve my skill set as progressed. There was an increase in learning since week 7 of this placement as I had completed 70 percent of my project in that milestone and made each tool run successfully and simultaneously in Docker containers using Docker-Compose and Dockerfile. At the end of the placement, I completed 100 percent of my project by the deadline, and successfully integrated it with the front-end parts of other intern students and pushed it to GitHub repository. I think if I had allowed time for myself to develop strategies to fix the weakness of my transferable skills at the beginning of this placement, I would not be so stress about my assignment and project submissions and could have done them with a better result. On reflection, I could feel that I became industry ready and more confident in my coding techniques and transferable skills due to these 12 weeks of acquired knowledge and experience with Securestack Company.


Learning

I learnt that transferable skills are very crucial for my personal and professional development. As mentioned above, most of my problems had been solved when I developed strategies and improved my transferable skills. I will therefore continue to acquire more knowledge to prepare myself to face professional challenges, especially in the area of decision making, critical thinking, time management, and problem-solving. I want to see learning as part of me because learning new skills every day is a lifelong experience; and I will not only improve the quality of my coding techniques but also enhance my personal development to close the gap between theory and practice.


Additional Artefact For 7822ICT (Lifelong Learning)


When I was an undergraduate student majoring in accounting, I had to follow protocols, standards, and regulations; thus, I learnt by heart, and like other Asian students, the way I developed my skills over time was to remember the learning materials line by line because it was the only key to my university success as my exams and assignments contained only closed questions. As a result, I did not pay attention to the so-called critical thinking or design thinking. Put it in other words, I was a passive learner. I rather accepted accounting techniques that mention in the books than thought out of the box. After I graduated, I knew I was a good accountant, but I was never a professional one.


At the time I decided to change my career path to computer science, I felt something was not right with my learning approach and the way I was taught. Coming to this realization, I began searching for programs to initiate my new career path and earn my master’s degree here in Australia. As I sit down to reflect on my time in the computer science major, I am confident that I made the right decision to come to Australia. As a lifelong learner, I found it interesting how my learning philosophy has continued to evolve over here. I have over 580 courses on Udemy platform ranking from accountant and technology to public speaking and critical thinking. I think the number of the courses on the platform will continue to improve as there are fantastic skills out there that I have not yet learnt. Now, not only I focus on the technical skill sets in my field of study, but I also target more on transferable skills that could add more value to my work performances as well as learning outcomes. These transferable skills include critical thinking, decision making, time management, and problem-solving.


I believe that transferable skills are universally essential in the workplace because they contribute to the success of a team or organization. Taking problem-solving and critical thinking as an example, they are important in almost any job, and HR looks for them during the interview. What I am trying to say is I do not have to remember everything in the field of computer science to become a tech-savvy because the technology evolving so fast every day. What I learn today might be deprecated tomorrow, and who knows? For instance, UIkit framework is being replaced by SwiftUI to develop iOS. Thus, all I need is to think critically to help myself better understand the problems and to avoid any kind of negative belief and focus more on my strengths to find the solutions to the problems. This is what the employer wants to see, and it is how I prove that I can do the job even if I am not the perfect math of the job description.
